The purpose of this project is to assess whether more individuals ride the subway when it rains and to generate a linear model to predict the number of hourly entries given the features provided in the dataset.

After reading in the data from the provided csv file, we quickly inspect the data to determine the size of the data frame, specifically the number of entries as well as the provided information for each data point. For our initial analysis, we will only require two columns, the first corresponding the the entries hourly, and the second corresponding the the indicator variable, rain, which takes on values 0 and 1. A first pass assessment of the data indicates that approximately 78% of the data comes from entries where there is no rain.

For our analysis of we first look at the distribution of the data independent of rain. Because we are specifically interested in whether or not more individuals ride when it rains, we can utilize a one-sized test at a significance level of .05. To determine the appropriate test to use, we can plot the data as a histogram. Because the distribution is not normal, we choose to use a non-parametric test, specifically the Mann-Whitney U.

In our case, it would be reasonable to use a one sided test, because in our original question, we are specifically interested in whether subway ridership increases. If we were simply interested in whether there was a difference, we would use a 2-sided test.

To address our first question, we perform both Welch's T-test and the Mann-Whitney U Rank test. Given that the data is not normally distributed, the Mann-Whitney U would be more appropriate as it is more robust. However, both of the tests return a p value of less than .05, and therefore our result is statistically significant and the probability of our data/observation given there is no difference is less than 10^-5. Our analysis showed that for each data point, there is an average of 1845 people when there is no rain, and an average of 2028 when it rains. This is a an increase of 183 on average, or approximately 10%, when it rains.

For the next portion of the project, we use linear regression to predict the number of entries at a given station at a given time based on our weather data. Our implementation of this linear regression model uses the OLS from SciKit Learn.

For our particular model, we choose to use all of the data except the latitude and longitude information. In addition to the weather, the unit, date, and time are all useful variables to use in our model as they all affect the number of entries and will provide a baseline number of individuals. We also remove a number of variables that are redundant such as the date and time information. Furthermore, based on the information about each of the variables, we suspect that it is possible that certain features are correlated. In particular, the UNIT station column may contain information about the unit column, as each unit must be associated with a station. However, it is not possible to tell whether decoupling this information will improve our prediction without testing.

After training our model, we should that it returns an R^2 value of .544 using online a baseline prediction and .548 when incorporating weather information. These values suggest that best predictors for hourly subway ridership are factors such as the station and the time (hour and day). The addition of weather information provides a very slight increase in our ability to predict ridership, but the data alone does not provide much insight. Intuitively, this is reasonable, as stations in more high traffic or densely populated areas would generally have more riders. In addition, most of the traffic would also occur during the day when people are active. Lastly, we would expect to see that weekdays and weekends have different traffic patterns.

Reflection

In this instance, I believe that the data was sufficient to provide some insight into the NYC subway ridership. After the generating the preliminary models and visualizations, I reassess the factors that I believe contributed most to the model and attempted a final model that I believe would best predict our variable of interest. Ideally, I would have able to combine the place, time, and day features into a single feature that could be used to predict the the subway ridership for our data set. However, when attempting to do that, I received a memory error and instead chose to combine the two features that showed the most promise. The rationale for combining these 3 predictors together is that they are synergistic and therefore have interactions between the variables. The assumption for fitting a linear model is that the coefficient generated assumes all other factors are constant. However, in the case of subway data, one particular station may see a difference of 1000 people at a given station at a given day and time, while for another, there may be a difference of 10000. Therefore, it may be more beneficial to give a single prediction based on the combined factors of station, day, and time. The limitations of this however, are that given that we have only a month of data, for a particular instance of subway/day/time, we would only have approximately 4 data points. Therefore, in order to determine the effectiveness of the model, we would need more data and generate to generate a test set to prevent overfitting.

Nonetheless, with the remaining features, I generated a linear model with an R^2 value of .815. Due to the non-linearity of the data, this is significantly better than the previous model, where we look at station and time independently. In light of this, it may be plausible to simply use the conditional mean of entries/hour given station, date, and time to predict subway ridership given a set of predictors. Furthermore, the use of a linear model, also assumes that the variables of interest are normally distributed. In our case, we know that the variable we are attempting to predict is right-skewed, therefore it is possible that the predicted number of individuals riding the subway would be overestimated at the low end. This is most likely due to the fact that in our model, day is represented as an independent variable and the difference between a weekday and weekend is still very high. However, our model still explains a great deal of the variance associated with the data. To improve out model, it may be useful to transform the data with a log transformation.

Lastly, we look at the limitations of the data. In this particular instance, this data set represents observations of approximately 450 New York subway stations for a one month period. And although this data may generalize well, it is difficult to predict how well it will generalize outside this time period. We may see the say overall trend, however, the changing months/seasons, may also have some substantial effect on subway ridership.
